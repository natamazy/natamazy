✅ Yes – Everyone Should Learn How to Code

1. Essential Digital Literacy

Coding is becoming part of modern literacy. Being tech-aware means being able to read and write the language of the digital world.

Computational Thinking, 10 Years Later

Author: Jeannette Wing (2016)
Explores how coding and computational thinking are fundamental literacies, becoming as essential as reading and writing.

Growing Digital Literacy through Coding

Organization: UNESCO (2020, updated 2023)
Discusses global coding initiatives like Africa Code Week, enabling even remote schools to engage digitally.

2. Critical Thinking and Problem-Solving

Coding isn't just about syntax — it's about thinking logically and solving real problems step-by-step.

How Coding Enhances Problem-Solving

Source: India Today (2023)
Shows how learning to code improves logical reasoning and problem-solving skills, even across unrelated subjects.

Exploring the Relationship Between Coding and Problem Solving

Authors: Relkin & Bers (2020)
Academic study showing young children improved their problem-solving ability through learning coding and computational thinking.

3. Economic Empowerment and Job Market Advantage

In many industries, knowing how to code opens doors — or at least gives you a foot in.

Half of the Highest-Paying Jobs Require Coding

Source: MarketWatch (2017)
Based on analysis of 26 million job postings, coding skills are now part of many top-paying careers.

Will Learning to Code Help You Get a Job?

Author: Charlotte Seager, The Guardian (2015)
Discusses increasing demand for tech skills across industries — not just in tech-specific roles.

4. Fostering Innovation and Creativity

Coding is a creative act — you make apps, games, art, tools. You invent and build.

The Next Generation of Scratch

Author: Mitchel Resnick (2019)
Emphasizes how coding with Scratch helps kids think creatively and work collaboratively.

The Benefits of Teaching Kids to Code

Source: Boston College Magazine (2023)
Highlights coding as a form of creative expression that also supports emotional and social development.

5. Democratizing Access to Technology

When more people know how to code, fewer are left behind in the digital revolution.

Growing Digital Literacy through Coding

Organization: UNESCO (2023)
Shows how inclusive coding programs bring tech literacy to marginalized communities.

The Democratization of Education

Organization: Toptal (2018)
Describes global initiatives that break down access barriers to technology education.

6. Early Exposure Builds Future Competence

Like learning a language, starting early makes coding more intuitive and less intimidating later.

Coding Class, Then Naptime

Source: NPR (2015)
Examines the benefits of teaching coding in kindergarten — as early exposure sets the stage for long-term success.

Teaching Kids to Code

Source: Boston College Magazine (2023)
Focuses on early learning tools like ScratchJr to build foundational tech skills in young children.

❌ No – Not Everyone Needs to Learn How to Code

1. Not All Careers Require Coding

Many jobs simply don’t need programming. Forcing it on everyone may be a waste of time and energy.

Should Everybody Learn to Code?

Author: Esther Shein, Communications of the ACM (2014)
Argues that not all careers benefit from coding; computational thinking may suffice for many roles.

Mandatory Coding May Not Be Necessary

Author: Patrick Gray, TechRepublic (2016)
Suggests that while coding is useful, it shouldn’t be required for students uninterested in tech.

2. One-Size-Fits-All Education Is Ineffective

Forcing coding on everyone doesn’t work — schools and students have different needs and resources.

Moving the Needle on Equity in CS Education

Organization: Urban Institute (2023)
Highlights flaws in CS-for-all programs like CS4All in NYC, where implementation has been deeply uneven.

3. Better Alternatives for Many Learners

Coding isn’t the only way to teach logic, creativity, or digital skills.

Why Children Should Not Be Taught to Code

Author: Prof. David Buckingham (2015)
Argues that the cognitive benefits of coding are overstated; many other activities teach similar skills.

4. Technological Abstraction Reduces Need

With AI and no-code tools, people can build tech without traditional programming.

Jensen Huang: Let AI Handle Coding

Author: Mark Tyson (2024)
Nvidia CEO argues AI tools are making coding unnecessary for most users.

5. Equity and Resource Issues

Not all schools have the tools or teachers needed to offer quality CS education to every student.

Equity-Focused Challenges in CS

Organization: CSTA (2024)
Details the gaps in teacher training and infrastructure that hinder equitable coding education.

6. Cultural and Educational Overreach

Coding is sometimes pushed too hard — to the point of ignoring culture, context, or creative fields.

Why Children Should Not Be Taught to Code

Author: David Buckingham (2015)
Criticizes the ideological push for coding in schools at the expense of arts and culture-based learning.

Colleges Shouldn’t Overfocus on Technical Skills

Author: Ben Kennedy (2020)
Warns that overemphasizing coding could narrow students’ education and deepen inequality.
